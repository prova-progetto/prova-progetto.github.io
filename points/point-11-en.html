Historical context

We are now in the heart of the lower city, a space that took on a central role in Bergamo‚Äôs urban development and public life during the twentieth century. At the beginning of the century, in fact, based on a project by architect Marcello Piacentini, city life gradually began to shift from the upper city toward the area where we are today.

The square acquired a strong symbolic and civic value, becoming a privileged place for celebrations, demonstrations, and moments of public gathering. Starting in 1921, it was officially named after Vittorio Veneto, taking on the meaning of a space dedicated to the memory of Bergamo‚Äôs fallen soldiers of the First World War and becoming an important place of collective memory.

The square takes its name from the Battle of Vittorio Veneto (24 October ‚Äì 4 November 1918), which marked Italy‚Äôs final victory over the Austro-Hungarian Empire in the First World War. This event was considered decisive for the end of the conflict and became a symbol of sacrifice, heroism, and national unity.

Why it is a place of memory

On 27 October 1924, a date linked to the anniversary of the March on Rome, Benito Mussolini inaugurated the tower that still dominates the square today, transforming it into a true celebratory monument of the regime. During the inauguration, speeches were given, in addition to the Duce himself, by Antonio Locatelli and Giacomo Suardo, leading figures of local and national fascism. In 1936, following the defeat in East Africa, the names of eight fallen soldiers were engraved on the tower, among whom also appears that of Locatelli, further strengthening the link between the monument and the fascist rhetoric of sacrifice and heroism.

Giacomo Suardo was one of the main figures of fascism in Bergamo. He held the role of local secretary of the National Fascist Party and was also a member of parliament, representing the connection between local and national fascist power.

Inside the tower there is also a sculptural portrait in his honor.

However, the meaning of the tower and the square changed over time, especially after 8 September 1943. Following the attempt at antifascist liberation, speeches were delivered from the tower by Ernesto Rossi, Luigi Bruno, and Mario Mammuccari, symbolically marking a break with the former regime. Two months later, a group of women decided to express their dissent toward the Nazi-Fascists through a simple but highly symbolic gesture: placing and throwing flowers at the base of the tower. After the liberation between 25 and 28 April 1945, partisan formations, instead of speaking from the tower‚Äôs balcony as the Duce had done, consciously chose to set up a raised stage in the square, positioning themselves almost at the same level as the people. This gesture emphasized the desire to break with the regime‚Äôs authoritarian rhetoric and to affirm a new relationship between citizens and power, based on participation and closeness.

In-depth ‚Äì ‚ÄúThe Battle of the Chrysanthemums‚Äù

On 4 November 1943, a group of women from Bergamo carried out a symbolic act of great courage and protest against the fascist regime. The women ‚Äì including Adriana De Leidi, Mary Tadini Leidi, Mimma Quarti, Velia Sacchi, Bianca Artifoni, Pina Callegari, Iginia and Emma Coggiola ‚Äì went to the base of the tower in Piazza Vittorio Veneto to lay flowers in memory of the fallen soldiers of the Great War.

A day commemorating the Italian victory in the First World War.

The police attempted to prevent the laying of flowers by protecting the tower; the women responded with determination, throwing the flowers over the heads of the officers, thus transforming a commemorative act into a public and symbolic act of dissent against the fascist regime.

The following year, the same gesture was repeated thanks to the organization of the Italian Communist Party (PCI), demonstrating how women‚Äôs protest in Bergamo had become part of a broader antifascist movement in the city.

To commemorate this important episode, which unites the memory of the fallen with civil resistance, Bergamo placed a commemorative plaque inside the tower in Piazza Vittorio Veneto, allowing citizens and visitors today to learn about the courage of those women who openly challenged fascist authority with a simple but powerful gesture.
