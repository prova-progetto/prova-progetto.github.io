The Special Tribunal was established by the fascist regime on November 25, 1926, and began operating in 1927. After an initial suppression in July 1943, it was re-established by the Italian Social Republic and remained operational until the Liberation, with a branch also in Bergamo.

The stabilization of the 1930s

In the 1930s, the Special Tribunal of Bergamo played a central role in stabilizing fascist justice, especially thanks to the 1931 Extension Law.

Its powers expanded: the tribunal was no longer limited to repressing anti-fascism, but became more broadly involved in defending the personality of the State and the security of the regime.

Although it represented the judicial peak of political repression, the tribunal did not hold a monopoly in this area but operated within a multi-centered system alongside the Ministry of the Interior, the police, the carabinieri, and the fascist party, with frequent overlapping of responsibilities. Within this system, the Special Tribunal of Bergamo had a selective function: it judged mainly the most serious and symbolically significant political crimes, while daily and preventive control remained with the other bodies. In this way, it helped make political repression an ordinary and stable element of the fascist legal system, strengthening the consolidation of the regime.

Why it is a place of memory

The Special Tribunal represents one of the most repressive tools of the fascist dictatorship. Created after the introduction of the ‚Äúfascist laws,‚Äù it was used to target any form of political opposition, erasing fundamental freedoms such as freedom of the press, association, and the right to strike.

Further insight: after the Liberation

After the fall of fascism and the end of World War II, places like this lost their repressive function. In the climate of democratic reconstruction of the Italian Republic, the building was repurposed for educational use, becoming the headquarters of the Artistic High School, founded in the early 1960s. The decision to transform a place linked to control and repression into a school reflected the desire to return these spaces to the community and to affirm new values based on education, culture, and freedom.

The subsequent naming of the school after Giacomo and Pio Manz√π reinforces the symbolic meaning of this transformation: from the headquarters of a tribunal that limited thought to a place dedicated to creativity and artistic expression. Today, studying in that building means inhabiting a space that tells the story of Italy‚Äôs transition from dictatorship to democracy, turning a place of repression into a place of formation and freedom.
