Reaching Piazza Giacomo Matteotti, we come across the town hall building known as Palazzo Frizzoni.

This building, a symbol of municipal power, became the official seat of the city administration on October 28, 1928 (the anniversary of the March on Rome ), replacing the former town hall located at Via T. Tasso, 4. The choice of this date was not accidental: October 28, 1928 marked the celebration of Mussolini‚Äôs rise to power and of the fascist regime, and represented a moment of consolidation of central government control over local administrations. Palazzo Frizzoni thus became the symbolic place of fascist power in the city. At the head of the municipal administration was the podest√†, a figure who replaced the mayor, and one of the most well-known podest√†s of that period was Antonio Locatelli, who held the office during those years.

Why this is a place of memory

From February 4, 1926, all the functions of mayor, municipal executive committee, and city council were centralized and assigned to the podest√†, a figure inspired by medieval models of centralized government, admired by Mussolini and reinterpreted as instruments of autocracy and control. This reform gave the podest√† exclusive power to appoint and manage municipal offices, eliminating all forms of popular participation.

The podest√† remained in office for a period of five years, but a subsequent fascist law established that only married men could hold this position, excluding Antonio Locatelli from the possibility of continuing his mandate. This episode represents an emblematic example of the punitive and rigid regulations imposed by fascism, which set not only political but also social and personal rules, within a logic of total control over private lives. Palazzo Frizzoni thus stands as a witness to an era in which local autonomy was replaced by the fascist dictatorship.

The municipality is no longer an expression of the citizens, but an instrument of the fascist state.

In-depth ‚Äì Antonio Locatelli

Antonio Locatelli was born in 1895 and took part in the First World War as an aviator, distinguishing himself for his courage and flying skills. The war experience deeply marked his life and helped strengthen his bond with nationalist ideology, which in the following years led him to enthusiastically embrace fascism.

During the rise of the fascist regime, Locatelli began a political career: between 1924 and 1928 he was elected as a deputy to the Parliament of the Kingdom of Italy. In 1933 he began his activity in Bergamo by taking on the role of podest√†. However, his mandate lasted only one year: in 1934, following a law wanted by Mussolini that prevented unmarried men from holding public office, Locatelli was forced to leave the position.

On January 7, 1936, he volunteered for the war in Ethiopia, taking part in military operations as an aircraft pilot. During the conflict, despite the technological and military superiority of the Italian army, Ethiopian forces managed to organize effective resistance actions. On a night in June 1936, the Ethiopians attacked an Italian airfield, setting the aircraft on fire: all the soldiers present were killed in the attack, including Antonio Locatelli.

His death was immediately celebrated by the regime as a heroic sacrifice. In Bergamo, a true cult of the hero developed, fueled by fascist propaganda, which presented Locatelli as a symbol of courage, dedication to the homeland, and loyalty to fascism. Even today, in the tower located in Piazza Vittorio Veneto, Antonio Locatelli is remembered through a sculpted portrait, a testimony to historical memory and to the significance his figure had for the city.

In-depth ‚Äì The myth of Ancient Rome in fascist rhetoric

The myth of Ancient Rome represented one of the most powerful ideological tools through which the fascist regime sought to legitimize its political and imperial project. The very term ‚Äúpodest√†‚Äù refers to a medieval office, in which an external magistrate, elected annually, governed the communes by holding executive, judicial, and military powers in order to ensure impartiality and order, replacing the consuls. Roman heritage was not presented as a simple cultural legacy, but as the natural destiny of the nation, called upon to reaffirm its dominance in the Mediterranean and in the world.

This operation directly involved the educational system. From the 1930s onward, schools became a central tool of indoctrination: Roman history was used as a pretext to exalt the imperial values of fascism, while a Roman-centered vision gave way to an openly Italy-centered perspective, in which fascist Italy appeared as the legitimate heir of the Roman Empire.

The reference to Roman heritage was also intertwined with the regime‚Äôs colonial and demographic project. The idea of the Empire, supported by the rhetoric of the ‚Äúcivilizing mission,‚Äù justified both the conquest of African colonies and the need to shape a ‚Äúnew Italian,‚Äù disciplined, loyal, and ready for sacrifice.

Through schools, families, mass media, and public space, the regime thus built an imaginary world in which Ancient Rome, fascism, and the colonial empire appeared as a single entity.
