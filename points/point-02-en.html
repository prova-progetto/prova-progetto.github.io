Point 02 – In memory of Ernesto and Ada Rossi

Memory Routes 2.0

Historical context

We are standing in front of the historic headquarters of the Vittorio Emanuele II Technical Institute. During the Fascist period, this building was not only a place of education, but also a center of surveillance and control. The most radical transformation occurred during the Nazi occupation (1943–1945): the building was seized by German forces and turned into a strategic military headquarters. Because of the occupation, educational activities were suspended and the school was forced to relocate, leaving these classrooms under military control until Liberation.

Why this is a place of memory

Ernesto Rossi turned his economics lectures at Vittorio Emanuele into a form of political resistance. A leader of the Justice and Freedom movement, he used Bergamo as a strategic hub to connect internal resistance with anti-fascist forces in exile. He paid for this courage with his arrest in 1930 and thirteen years of imprisonment, during which he contributed to the drafting of the Ventotene Manifesto, laying the foundations for a united Europe.

Ada Rossi, a scientist and fiercely independent woman, was a key figure in the city’s civil resistance. Dismissed from her job by the regime, she never stopped fighting: through private lessons, she educated dozens of young people in democratic values, preparing the ground for the future local Resistance. Acting as a silent courier, she helped smuggle out of confinement the writings that would shape European identity.

Together, Ernesto and Ada Rossi demonstrated that freedom is defended both through political action and the education of younger generations.

The more than 900 letters exchanged during imprisonment were not merely personal messages, but real instruments of resistance. Ada secretly read them to her students to educate them in freedom: “In Bergamo, thirty people read them and are amazed by your courage.” In those writings, Ernesto insisted that “no defeat is final as long as one refuses to accept it,” transforming the suffering of imprisonment into a shared civic consciousness for the entire city.
