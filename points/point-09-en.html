Historical context

Continuing along Via T. Tasso, you reach Piazza Vittorio Veneto, where the ninth of the 17 points of our journey is located: the Employment Offices.

Public bodies responsible for managing the hiring of workers. During fascism, they became tools of political control, through which the regime decided who was allowed to work.

During the Italian Social Republic ( RSI ), these offices took on a central role in strengthening fascist control over the country‚Äôs economic and social life. Several offices were established here to manage and allocate labor, with the aim of ensuring that hiring followed fascist criteria and that workers were aligned with the ideals of the regime.

Fascist state established in Northern Italy between 1943 and 1945 after the armistice of September 8. It was led by Benito Mussolini and militarily supported by Nazi Germany.

Why this is a place of memory

Fascist control over workplaces was not limited to bureaucratic organization, but also resulted in discrimination against those who did not follow the regime. Starting on March 29, 1928, fascism began to intervene directly in workplaces through a Royal Decree, which established that hiring should give priority to those holding a membership card of the National Fascist Party (PNF). The law not only imposed ideological selection, but also helped exclude from employment those who were not loyal to the regime.

A measure issued by the King on proposal of the fascist government, introducing direct state intervention in labor relations and favoring workers registered with the party.

A document certifying membership in the Fascist Party, often essential to obtain or keep a job and to access public positions.

In-depth ‚Äì The General Labour Inspectorate

In addition to the employment offices, the General Labour Inspectorate also operated in this area, an institution established and managed by the Italian Social Republic. This body mainly had two tasks.

The first was to replace conscripted workers with so-called volunteer workers. The Inspectorate sought to limit the direct use of coercion by offering ‚Äúvoluntary‚Äù labor in place of requisitioned labor. However, this voluntariness was often only apparent, as many accepted out of necessity, fear of reprisals, or to avoid deportation.

German authorities imposed conscription, meaning the obligation to report for forced labor, with no possibility of refusal.

Constraint or oppression that forces someone to act against their own will.

The second objective was direct cooperation with German authorities for the execution of infrastructural and strategic works, such as the construction and repair of roads, bridges, and railways, as well as other civil and military projects essential to the war effort and territorial control.

In this context, the Organisation Todt was also active, a German organization responsible for major military and infrastructural works in occupied territories during the war. In the province of Bergamo, Todt conscripted 6,848 workers, but only 146 actually reported for duty; of these, 13 were sent to work in Germany.

The harshness of repression is still remembered today by a plaque located in Via Porta Dipinta, in Bergamo, commemorating five citizens from Bergamo who were executed in Pesaro, victims of violence and reprisals linked to the system of occupation and collaboration.
